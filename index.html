Formula Sheet

All key rules, equations, and comparisons for Unit 4.

⭐ Universal Profit-Max Rule

⭐
MR = MC → Find Q*, then P* from DemandEvery market structure maximizes profit at MR=MC. The firm then finds the price consumers will pay at Q* by going UP to the demand curve.
Profit = TR − TC = (P − ATC) × Q*
TR = P × Q  |  MR = ΔTR/ΔQ

Why MR < P for Imperfect Competition

Facing a downward-sloping demand curve, a firm must lower price on ALL units to sell one more. The revenue gained from the new unit is reduced by the price cut on existing units. So MR = New Price − (Price Drop × Old Quantity) < New Price.

📊 Efficiency Standards

Allocative Efficiency: P = MC

Resources flow to their highest-valued use. Society's marginal benefit (P) equals marginal cost. This is the socially optimal quantity. Only perfect competition achieves this in LR.

Productive Efficiency: P = min ATC

Firm minimizes cost per unit — operating at the bottom of the ATC curve. Only perfect competition achieves this in LR.

TR Maximization: MR = 0

Total revenue is at its maximum when one more unit adds nothing to revenue. This is NOT the profit-max point (unless MC = 0).

Break-Even / Fair Return: P = ATC

Zero economic profit. Commonly used for natural monopoly "fair return pricing" regulation. Firm stays in business but earns no above-normal returns.

🎭 Price Discrimination (Perfect/1st Degree)

Key Features of Perfect PD

  • D = MR: Demand curve becomes the MR curve (no need to cut price on previous units)
  • Consumer surplus = 0 (firm captures all)
  • DWL = 0 (firm produces allocatively efficient quantity)
  • Profit = maximum possible
  • Conditions required: market power + separate buyers by elasticity + prevent resale

🏪 Monopolistic Competition LR Equilibrium

P = ATC > MC
  • P = ATC → zero economic profit (demand tangent to ATC)
  • P > MC → NOT allocatively efficient (unlike PC)
  • Excess capacity → produces left of min-ATC point
  • If SR profit → firms enter → demand shifts left → profit → 0
  • If SR loss → firms exit → demand shifts right → loss → 0

♟️ Game Theory — How to Solve a Payoff Matrix

1
For each player, go row by row (or column by column): Given the rival's choice, which of your strategies gives the higher payoff? Circle it.
2
If ALL your circles are in the same row/column: That is your dominant strategy — you should always do this regardless of rival.
3
If your circles are in different rows/columns: You have a dependent strategy — your best choice depends on what the rival does.
4
Find Nash Equilibrium: Look for a cell where BOTH players' circles land. Neither player wants to switch — this is the Nash equilibrium. If two circles are in the same cell → Nash equilibrium found.
4.1–4.2

Monopoly

One seller, high barriers to entry, downward-sloping demand, persistent long-run profit.

⭐
The Profit-Max Rule: MR = MCAll profit-maximizing firms produce where MR = MC. Then price from the demand curve (go UP from Q* to Demand). The demand curve is ALWAYS above MR for imperfect competitors.

Why is MR < Price?

A monopolist faces the entire market's downward-sloping demand. To sell one extra unit, the firm must lower price — and that lower price applies to every unit already being sold. So MR = (New Price) − (Price Drop × Previous Quantity) < New Price.

Example: Selling 10 units at $80, or 11 units at $78. MR = $78 − ($2 × 10) = $78 − $20 = $58. But price = $78. So MR < P.

MR Curve Properties (each labeled ①–⑥ on graph below ↓)

  1. Same y-intercept as demand — both start at same price on P-axis
  2. Twice as steep as demand — MR slope = 2 × demand slope
  3. Hits x-axis at ½ the Q where demand crosses (if D hits Q=50, MR hits Q=25)
  4. MR > 0 → elastic range — TR is still rising; monopolist never prices here
  5. MR = 0 → unit elastic — TR is at its maximum; MR crosses x-axis
  6. MR < 0 → inelastic range — TR is falling; monopolist never prices here

Barriers to Entry (Source of Monopoly Power)

  • Patents and copyrights (legal protection)
  • Ownership of key raw materials
  • Economies of scale → Natural Monopoly
  • Government licensing / franchises

These barriers allow long-run economic profit to persist — unlike competitive markets where entry eliminates profit.

Allocative Inefficiency & Deadweight Loss

A monopolist produces where MR=MC, meaning P > MC. Society values extra units (at the demand price P) more than they cost to produce (MC) — but the monopolist won't produce them. This "foregone surplus" is the Deadweight Loss (DWL) triangle between Q* and the competitive quantity Qc (where D=MC).

Natural Monopoly & Regulation

When LRATC declines throughout the entire relevant demand range, one firm can serve the whole market at lower average cost than multiple firms.

Regulation options:

  • Socially optimal (P=MC): allocatively efficient but causes a loss → needs government subsidy
  • Fair return (P=ATC): break-even, zero economic profit — most common
  • Unregulated: profit-max price, DWL exists, but firm stays viable

How to Read a Monopoly Graph (Step by Step)

1

2

3

4.3

Price Discrimination

Charging different prices to different consumers based on willingness to pay — capturing consumer surplus as profit.

What Is Price Discrimination?

Selling the same product at different prices to different consumers based on their demand elasticity or willingness to pay. Firms use this to capture consumer surplus as profit.

Three conditions required:

  • Market power (face downward-sloping demand)
  • Ability to identify and separate buyers by demand elasticity
  • Ability to prevent resale (arbitrage between groups)

Real-World Examples

  • Airline ticket pricing (same seat, vastly different prices)
  • Movie theater discounts (students, seniors, children)
  • Software: student vs. professional licenses
  • Coupons and loyalty discounts
  • Pharmaceutical pricing across countries

Perfect (1st Degree) Price Discrimination

The firm charges each consumer exactly their maximum willingness to pay:

  • D = MR: The demand curve itself becomes the MR curve (no need to cut price on previous units, so each unit adds its full price to revenue)
  • Consumer surplus = 0 (all captured as producer surplus)
  • DWL = 0 (produces allocatively efficient quantity — same Q as competitive market)
  • Profit = maximum possible

Key Takeaway

Under perfect price discrimination, the firm charges every consumer their exact WTP. The demand curve becomes the MR curve because each additional unit can be sold at a different (lower) price without affecting the price on previous units. This eliminates both consumer surplus and deadweight loss — all welfare is captured as the firm's profit. Output equals the competitive level.

4.4

Monopolistic Competition

Many firms, differentiated products, free entry → zero long-run economic profit. The most realistic and common market structure.

Characteristics (from your teacher's notes)

  • Many firms — each too small to significantly affect others
  • Differentiated products — real or imagined differences (quality, location, branding, packaging). Is Coors really different than Miller? Is a Ford F-150 really different than a Chevy Silverado?
  • Low/no barriers to entry — free entry and exit
  • Advertising — firms advertise to create brand loyalty and perceived differences
  • Examples: restaurants, hair salons, clothing stores, fast food chains
🔄
Long-Run Adjustment Process
SR Profit → New firms enter with similar products → each existing firm loses customers → demand shifts left → profit → zero.
SR Loss → Firms exit → remaining firms gain customers → demand shifts right → loss → zero.

Excess Capacity

In LR equilibrium, the firm produces on the downward-sloping portion of ATC — to the left of the minimum-ATC quantity. The firm could produce more at lower average cost. This "slack" is excess capacity. Perfect competition produces at min ATC (efficient). All imperfect competition structures have excess capacity.

Long-Run Equilibrium Conditions

  • P = ATC → zero economic profit (demand is tangent to ATC)
  • P > MC → NOT allocatively efficient (unlike PC where P=MC)
  • P > min ATC → NOT productively efficient (excess capacity)
  • Demand curve is elastic (but less than perfect competition)

From Your Teacher: Product Differentiation

"Each firm in Mon. Comp. pretends to be a monopoly in its niche — Crest: is the only one, no other product is the same. Prell: no other product is like it."

Sources of product differentiation: quality, packaging, supplementary service, location, brand name & advertising.

To prevent long-run equilibrium: firms change product, advertising, quality, or packaging regularly ("New and Improved") to stay ahead of comparisons.

Short Run: Economic Profit

In the short run, existing firms can earn economic profit (P > ATC at Q*). This is temporary — new firms will enter and shift this firm's demand curve leftward until profit is eliminated.

4.5

Oligopoly & Game Theory

Few large interdependent firms — every decision depends on what rivals do. Analyzed using payoff matrices.

Oligopoly Characteristics

  • Few large firms (2–10) dominating the market
  • Mutual interdependence: each firm's decisions depend on rivals' reactions
  • Significant barriers to entry
  • Homogeneous or differentiated products
  • Examples: airlines, auto manufacturers, streaming services, soft drinks

Game Theory Vocabulary

  • Dominant strategy: best response regardless of what rival does ("always do this")
  • Dominated strategy: never the best response ("never do this")
  • Dependent strategy: best response depends on rival (no dominant strategy)
  • Nash equilibrium: outcome where neither player benefits from switching unilaterally
  • Prisoner's Dilemma: both have dominant strategy to defect, leading to worse outcome than cooperation

Collusion & Cartel Instability

Firms agree to act jointly (like a monopoly) to maximize joint profit. But:

  • Each firm has incentive to secretly cheat (undercut price, grab market share)
  • If all others cooperate, cheating earns you more
  • This creates a Prisoner's Dilemma — dominant strategy is to defect
  • Cartels are illegal in the U.S. (antitrust law)

From Your Teacher's AP Questions

Attaburger/Beefsteak: Two fast-food restaurants can price High or Low. Nash = Low/Low ($12/$8). Collusion at High/High ($23 joint). Attaburger has incentive to cheat (gets $12 > collusion amount). Beefsteak has no dominant strategy.

Bender/Jibson guitar makers: Bender's dominant strategy = Cheat. Jibson's dominant strategy = Not Cheat. Nash equilibrium = Bender cheats, Jibson doesn't cheat → $25k/$20k.

Natural Monopoly — LRATC Declines Throughout Demand Range

Three Regulation Outcomes

  • Unregulated P* (profit-max): Firm earns economic profit. Creates DWL. No subsidy needed.
  • Fair Return — P = ATC: Zero economic profit. Most common AP regulation. Firm stays viable. Some DWL remains.
  • Socially Optimal — P = MC: Allocatively efficient (DWL=0) but P < ATC → firm takes a loss → needs government subsidy.

Why "Natural" Monopoly?

LRATC keeps falling over the entire relevant demand range — economies of scale are so strong that one firm can serve the whole market at lower average cost than two or more firms could. Breaking it up would increase average costs. Examples: water utilities, electricity grids, natural gas pipelines.

Consumer Surplus, Profit, and DWL on One Graph

Reading the Areas

  • Consumer Surplus (CS): Triangle above P*, below Demand curve, left of Q*. What consumers gain from paying less than their WTP.
  • Profit rectangle: Area between P* (top), ATC* (bottom), from Q=0 to Q*. Economic profit per unit × Q*.
  • Deadweight Loss (DWL): Triangle between Q* and Qc (competitive output), bounded by Demand above and MC below. Welfare destroyed by underproduction.

Key: Why Does DWL Exist?

For every unit between Q* and Qc, a consumer is willing to pay (shown by demand) more than it costs to produce (shown by MC). Those trades don't happen because the monopolist restricts output. That foregone surplus is the DWL.

Monopolistic Competition: Short-Run vs. Long-Run Outcomes
Short Run: Profit
D (elastic, sloped) MR MC ATC
P* > ATC at Q*. Firm acts like a mini-monopolist. This profit is temporary — new firms will enter with differentiated products, shifting this firm's demand left until profit = 0.
Short Run: Loss
D (elastic, sloped) MR MC ATC
P* < ATC at Q*. Firm loses money. This is also temporary — firms will exit, shifting remaining firms' demand right until losses disappear.
Long Run: Tangency (P = ATC)
D (tangent to ATC) MR MC ATC
P = ATC (tangency) → zero economic profit. But P > MC (not allocatively efficient). Firm produces with excess capacity — Q* is left of min-ATC.

Long-Run Adjustment: Entry and Exit

Profit → Entry → Demand Shifts Left

When SR profit exists, new firms enter with similar differentiated products. Each existing firm loses market share → demand curve shifts leftward. This compresses the profit rectangle until demand is tangent to ATC.

Loss → Exit → Demand Shifts Right

When SR losses exist, firms exit the market. Remaining firms gain customers → demand curve shifts rightward. This narrows the loss rectangle until demand is tangent to ATC from below.

Long-Run Conditions (Memorize These)

  • P = ATC → zero economic profit
  • P > MC → not allocatively efficient
  • Q* < min-ATC Q → excess capacity
  • Demand is tangent to ATC at Q*
Perfect Competition: The firm is a price taker — Demand = MR = Price (horizontal line)
Short Run: Profit
D=MR=P (horizontal) MC ATC AVC
P > ATC at Q* where P=MC. Profit = green rectangle. New firms will enter in the long run, increasing supply, lowering market price until profit = 0.
Short Run: Loss (Stay Open)

Long Run: P = min ATC
D=MR=P (horizontal) MC ATC AVC
P = MC = min ATC. Zero economic profit. Allocatively efficient (P=MC) AND productively efficient (P=min ATC). The only structure achieving both. No excess capacity.

Shutdown Rule vs. Continue Operating

Shutdown Decision (Short Run)

  • If P ≥ AVC: Stay open. Revenue covers variable costs, and any excess helps pay fixed costs. Losses are smaller than if the firm shut down.
  • If P < AVC: Shut down immediately. Can't even cover variable costs — operating makes losses worse than just absorbing fixed costs.
  • If P < ATC but P > AVC: Stay open in SR, but exit in the long run when fixed costs (leases, contracts) can be adjusted.

Long-Run Equilibrium — Perfect Competition is Unique

Only perfect competition achieves both allocative efficiency (P=MC) and productive efficiency (P=min ATC) in the long run. All four conditions hold simultaneously: P = MC = min ATC = MR.

Graph Description (memorize the setup)

Official 2011 AP® Scoring Guidelines

PartPointsAnswer
(a)1 ptProfit-maximizing price = $24 (read from demand curve at Q where MR=MC; $22 also acceptable)
(b)1 ptProfit per unit at Q=8 = P − ATC = $24 − $18 = $6
(c)1 ptAllocative efficiency is NOT achieved because price ≠ marginal cost (P > MC at the profit-maximizing output)
(d)1 ptDemand is inelastic between $16 and $18 — because total revenue increases as price rises (TR rises when P rises → inelastic); equivalently, MR is negative in this range
(e-i)1 ptThe monopolist is NOT earning positive economic profit at Q=11 — because at Q=11, P = ATC (break-even point on the graph)
(e-ii)1 ptThe monopolist IS earning positive accounting profit — economic profit = 0 means all economic costs covered, but accounting profit doesn't subtract opportunity cost (normal profit)
(f-i)1 ptMR of the 8th unit under a $22 price ceiling = $22 (the price ceiling becomes the new MR for units up to where demand crosses the ceiling)
(f-ii)1 ptQuantity produced = 9 units (at Q=9, price ceiling $22 = MC)
(g-i)1 ptUnder perfect price discrimination, quantity = 10 units (firm produces until last unit's WTP = MC, i.e., where demand meets MC)
(g-ii)1 ptConsumer surplus = $0 (zero) — firm captures all consumer surplus as profit by charging each consumer their exact WTP

Key Insight from This FRQ

Part (d) is a classic trap: the question asks about $16→$18, which is in the lower part of the demand curve. Remember: the upper half of any linear demand curve is elastic, the lower half is inelastic. A monopolist profit-maximizes in the elastic range, but CAN'T prevent consumers from being in the inelastic range at other prices.

Scanned Textbook — Monopoly FRQ

Grant's Gas Guzzlers is a used car lot operating as a geographic monopoly. It is currently producing despite having economic losses.

a
Why might Grant's Gas Guzzlers remain open despite the economic loss?
b
Assume Grant's is now earning economic profits. (i) At the profit-maximizing price, is demand elastic, unit elastic, or inelastic? (ii) If Grant's increases its prices, what happens to total revenue?
c
Assume Grant's fixed costs increase. What happens to its profit-maximizing quantity? Explain.

Answer Key (from scanned solutions)

(a) At this profit-maximizing level of output, price must be above average variable cost. At this price the firm can at least pay all variable costs and some fixed costs by staying in business. Shutting down would require paying all fixed costs with zero revenue — a worse outcome.

(b)(i) Elastic range. Monopolies always produce on the elastic portion of their demand curve (where MR > 0, because MR = MC > 0).

(b)(ii) Total revenue would decrease. In the elastic range, price and total revenue move in opposite directions. Increasing price reduces TR.

(c) The profit-maximizing quantity remains the same. Fixed costs don't affect marginal cost or marginal revenue — the two curves used to find Q*. Fixed costs only change total cost and ATC, shifting the profit rectangle but not the optimal quantity.

2004 AP® Microeconomics Exam — Question 3
8 points (4+4)

A profit-maximizing firm in a monopolistically competitive industry is in long-run equilibrium.

a
Draw a correctly labeled graph showing the profit-maximizing firm's price and output in long-run equilibrium.

Official 2004 AP® Scoring Guidelines

PartPointsAnswer
(a)4 pts(1) Downward-sloping demand with MR below it; (2) Q at MR=MC; (3) P from demand above Q; (4) ATC tangent to demand at Q — this is the LR tangency condition showing P = ATC
(b-i)2 ptsOutput does NOT change. The license fee is a fixed cost — it does not affect marginal cost. Since MR and MC are unchanged, the profit-maximizing output is unchanged.
(b-ii)2 ptsEconomic profits increase. When fixed costs fall, ATC decreases (ATC = AFC + AVC, and AFC falls). Lower ATC means a larger profit rectangle at the same Q* and P*.

Common Mistake

Students often say output increases because the firm is "more profitable." But fixed costs are SUNK in the short run — they don't change the MR=MC intersection. The quantity and price are unchanged; only the profit rectangle gets larger.

d

Answer Key (from scanned solutions)

(a) Oligopoly — only two firms in the market (duopoly), interdependent pricing decisions.

(b) No dominant strategy for Amazing Jake's. Jake's best move depends on Fields' choice: If Jake's goes High when Fields' goes High → Jake earns $35 (vs $30 if Low); if Jake's goes Low when Fields' goes Low → Jake earns $20 (vs $15 if High). So if Fields' goes High, Jake prefers High ($35 > $30). But if Fields' goes Low, Jake prefers Low ($20 > $15). Different best responses → no dominant strategy (dependent strategy).

(c) If Fields' goes Low → Jake goes Low (earns $20 vs $15).

(d) Nash equilibrium = Both High ($30, $35). Fields' dominant strategy is High (High always better: $30>$15 if Jake High; $20>$5 if Jake Low). Knowing Fields' goes High, Jake's best response is High ($35>$30). Both circles land at High/High → Nash equilibrium.
